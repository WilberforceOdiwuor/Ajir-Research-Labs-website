Intellectual Abdication Effect of AI and Internet and Its Role in the Erosion of Intelligence and the Rise of Algorithmic Radicalization

By Calvince Ademba, Lead AI Researcher, Ajir Research Labs

Abstract

This literature review investigates the phenomenon of intellectual abdication — the transfer of mental work to external systems — and its impact on the degradation of human intelligence and the ascent of algorithmic radicalization. As generative AI and LLMs become more embedded in people's lives, they offer an act of rapid offloading that shifts cognitive dispositions and disrupts the metacognitive friction needed to foster deep learning. Analysing the Distributed Atrophy Model, this review demonstrates the disengagement process as a cascade: cognitive offloading, followed by reduced representational strain, the loss of internal abstraction, and ultimately representational atrophy. By bypassing the generative processes for abstraction and inference central to learning, this progression contributes to digital amnesia and an erosion of the effort essential for critical cognition. The cognitive vacuum and epistemic stagnation that result render the individual an ideal subject for extremist manipulation. The review integrates findings on how radicalizing algorithms exploit this intellectual inertia by rewarding engagement over accuracy through behavioural analytics and targeted tailoring. It identifies a cultural syndrome in which passivity reproduces itself across broader social structures, resulting in moral abdication and an uncritical acceptance of algorithmic logic as truth.

1. Introduction

The Fourth Industrial Revolution is creating a new mode of working that will profoundly change how we learn and think, through intelligent AI systems capable of pattern recognition and autonomous decision-making [Velarde, 2019]. These systems — from virtual assistants to intricate decision support algorithms — have infiltrated every aspect of daily functioning, offering greater efficiency and seamless access to information. Yet as programs like Google Maps, Spotify, and Siri perform human tasks seamlessly, there is a pressing need to respect the unique cognitive, moral, and creative capacities that define the human being. Framing this technological shift around a positivist epistemology that displaces subjective and pluralistic systems of knowledge creates a deep tension between the efficiency of generative AI (GenAI) and the preservation of human intelligence [Hyland, 2023].

GenAI is capable of generating elaborate responses to complex problems, but at a price: people may compromise their critical thinking in exchange for speed and, ultimately, the metacognitive strategies central to reasoning [Sencer & Laura, 2023]. This outsourcing of thought — cognitive offloading — subtly reduces opportunities for challenge-based neuroplasticity, the very mechanism that allows cognitive resilience and growth [Jadhav, 2025]. Intellectual abdication is the passing of mental tasks to external systems that come to function as a secondary brain. It alters mental routines and progressively erodes internal learning mechanisms such as abstraction and rehearsal. When human thinking abandons the processes integral to intellectual development, there is a risk of epistemic stagnation, as humans become mired in machine-driven conformity [Zhang & Nauman, 2019]. This reliance on automated systems creates a cognitive vacuum that radicalizing algorithms are well-positioned to fill, observing online behaviour patterns to identify and target individuals predisposed to extremist ideologies.

2. The Psychology of Intellectual Abdication

The development and spread of Large Language Models has produced an immediacy of offloading that is distinctly different from traditional note-taking tools or search engines. Extended use of these systems may lead to distributed atrophy, a conceptual framework capturing how habitual LLM use results in a cascade of cognitive disengagement. The Distributed Atrophy Model comprises four distinct stages [Jadhav, 2025]:

  • Cognitive Offloading — Delegating tasks to LLMs reflects a broader cognitive strategy to reduce processing strain and generate outputs without mental struggle. When external tools are used solely to reduce effort, human users risk disengaging from the very processes of abstraction, rehearsal, and inference integral to deep understanding. Cognitive offloading therefore becomes not just a strategy, but a surrender.
  • Reduced Representational Strain — LLMs simulate fluent language and reasoning so effortlessly that they create an illusion of parity, where users perceive them as mental companions rather than tools. This misperception promotes cognitive complacency: the smooth fluency of the exchange bypasses crucial learning and internalisation mechanisms, weakening the architecture of independent thought.
  • Disuse of Internal Abstraction — Repeated reliance on external systems leads to the disuse of internal abstraction, where human users bypass the generative processes that underlie the synthesis and categorisation of information. This change is analogous to dimensionality reduction in machine learning, where complexity is sacrificed for efficiency. Individuals are more inclined to offload when GenAI interfaces are intuitive, non-effortful, and reactive — yielding an immediate performance boost while attenuating internal encoding.
  • Risk of Representational Atrophy — What begins as convenience can quietly calcify into dependence, undermining trust in one's own mind. This is an insidious disintegration of cognitive identity, where repeated reliance on external conversational systems leads to cognitive outsourcing so entrenched that users experience a form of withdrawal when denied access to the tool.

This cognitive neglect is described by the concept of the fluency trap, in which AI-generated content appears more fluent than human input but fails to reflect genuine intelligent process — relying on prior data exposure rather than novel problem-solving [Georgiou, 2025]. Physiological intelligence is dynamic, strongly dependent on neuroplasticity to reinforce pathways through repeated use. Continued reliance on algorithmic systems leads to digital neuroplasticity drift, dulling activation in the prefrontal cortex and affecting impulse control and deliberation [Firth et al., 2020]. Where AI substitutes rather than complements effort, gray matter thinning in regions responsible for synthesis has been observed, contributing to digital amnesia — a contemporary form of the Google Effect, in which the brain treats the internet as an external memory partner and struggles to retain information it knows it can easily access online [Faculty of Law, GLS University, Ahmedabad & Thaker, 2025].

3. The Impact on Human Intelligence and Discernment

The proliferation of AI in daily life has produced a measurable loss of slow, effortful thinking in favour of expedient, intuitive action. Critical thinking — the capacity to analyse, evaluate, and synthesise information — is gradually eroded by cognitive offloading. Research finds that individuals with high AI dependence exhibit less metacognitive friction: they produce faster but less original outputs and spend less time verifying conclusions. This shift encourages the rise of shallow knowledge workers, individuals skilled in the speed of manipulation but lacking synthesis, reflection, or ethical discernment [Gerlich, 2025].

This process diminishes epistemic agency. Users accustomed to AI-driven convenience are less likely to seek the reflective struggle required for deep learning. Cognitive Load Theory holds that while scaffolding prevents strain, simplistic automation undermines schema formation and transfer. True intelligence involves solving new problems; AI systems solve problems by repeating patterns from vast prior datasets, breeding complacency over originality [Faculty of Law, GLS University, Ahmedabad & Thaker, 2025].

The shift from information overload to filtered simplification further complicates human discernment. AI systems act as sociotechnical agents that shape perception and reasoning by incorporating cultural assumptions into their algorithmic logic. Constant notifications and filtered feeds fragment attention and reduce the capacity for sustained engagement with complex tasks, resulting in superficial processing. This overreliance also produces the black box problem — where humans cannot understand how AI decisions are made — diminishing critical engagement and responsibility. The result is epistemic flattening: the systemic replacement of diverse knowledge with quantifiable data fragments [Carabantes, 2020].

4. Algorithmic Radicalization

Radicalization is a process of belief and attitude change that typically culminates in the adoption of extreme or violent ideologies. This process has evolved into algorithmic cyber-radicalization, where extremist organizations capitalize on technological developments to promote ideology and sustain online communities [Muhammad Asad Latif, 2024]. Before generative AI, groups such as the Islamic State deployed automated Twitter and Telegram bots to distribute publications, supply propaganda archives, and engage in pseudo-human interactions — enabling extremist groups to circumvent censorship and operate globally with sophisticated media strategy [Alrhmoun et al., 2024].

AI accelerates and personalises this radicalization by prioritising engagement over accuracy. Systems equipped with behavioural analytics observe searching patterns, browsing history, and social interactions to predict which individuals are most susceptible to recruitment. Through algorithmic targeting, content is shifted to the right people based on their specific interests, emotions, and psychological states. Unlike generic messaging, AI can mine large volumes of personal data to generate propaganda that speaks directly to a user's fears, desires, and grievances, making it remarkably effective [McCurdy, 2021].

This process is reinforced by filter bubbles and echo chambers — digital spaces where AI surfaces content matching prior activity and suppresses opposing views, a phenomenon particularly visible in Instagram Reels and TikTok. These chambers ensure that extremist ideologies resonate with exceptional social force, creating a rabbit hole phenomenon in which progressively more extreme content is selected and served until the user's mental frame has been substantially shifted. The deployment of deepfakes and AI-generated misinformation further exploits existing social tensions by constructing a false version of reality that serves extremist promotion [Burton, 2023].

5. Intellectual Abdication as a Tool for Radicalization

Intellectual abdication serves as the ideal catalyst for this radicalization process. A person who has fully delegated their thinking to external AI tools is the ideal subject for an algorithm. Individuals who rely on AI for cognitive offloading experience a reduction in metacognitive engagement, making them less likely to detect algorithmic bias or misinformation. The frictionless nature of AI-generated information ensures that the critical guard is lowered, as content is passively consumed at speed [Burke, 2022].

AI's capacity to deliver easy, fast outcomes that validate pre-existing biases accelerates the radicalization process through algorithmic bias. Recommendation systems construct echo chambers by surfacing content aligned with existing beliefs, thereby reinforcing confirmation bias and reducing critical scrutiny. The opacity of AI processes leads users to accept AI-generated conclusions without examination, reducing engagement and promoting dependence on automated external reasoning [Kidd & Birhane, 2023].

This dynamic gives rise to a recurring cultural syndrome, where mental passivity extends into wider societal structures, establishing a loop of intellectual disengagement. As individuals become more cognitively passive, they gradually normalise algorithmic logic as their epistemic foundation. This constitutes a form of moral abdication — human agents abandoning their ethical responsibility to interrogate opaque algorithmic logic, accepting machine-generated rankings and ideologically inflected outputs without verification. As users become accustomed to the ease of AI-provided solutions, they lose the capacity for the reflective struggle necessary to challenge extremist narratives, rendering them highly vulnerable to ideological manipulation [Burke, 2022].

6. Conclusion and Future Directions

A dangerous link exists between cognitive ease and ideological extremism when we depend on AI to automate our mental tasks, undermining the cognitive resilience necessary to resist radicalization. The move from the Google Effect to the generative AI shift marks a transition from the outsourcing of memory to the outsourcing of reasoning and moral judgment. What the mind does not use, it will eventually lose, and cognitive atrophy — the inability of the human intellect to navigate ambiguity and novelty — undermines our collective capacity to meet the demands of the modern world.

Nevertheless, significant research gaps remain. The long-term impact of LLM use on learners' cognitive involvement and memory formation is not yet well-studied. There is a need to explore the nature of AI dependence — in particular, whether sustained reliance on these systems will arrest our capacity for autonomous judgment. Equally important is the examination of diagnostic and regulatory frameworks capable of maintaining epistemic integrity in education and policy, ensuring that AI functions as a complementary tool to augment human intelligence rather than a substitute for it.

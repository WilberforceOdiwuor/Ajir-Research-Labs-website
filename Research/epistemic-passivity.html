Epistemic Passivity

By Calvince Ademba, Lead AI Researcher, Ajir Research Labs

Abstract

This review collectively demystifies the complex relationship between artificial intelligence (AI), human cognition, and epistemic agency. It investigates how the increasing incorporation of generative AI (GenAI) into daily life, education, and professional work is restructuring how humans think, learn, and justify their beliefs [5] The review argues that however much AI offers immense potential for efficiency and personalised support, its misuse results in epistemic passivity and cognitive atrophy. The consensus suggests that maintaining human epistemic agency requires mindful engagement, critical oversight, and the use of AI as a tool for inquiry rather than an authoritative oracle that does away with the human intellectual autonomy.

Introduction

The integration of Generative Artificial Intelligence (GenAI) into educational and professional fields has initiated a significant shift in human cognitive habits. These tools have offered enhanced productivity and have personalized learning, however, they have simultaneously introduce a bigger risk of epistemic passivity [13].

Epistemic passivity is a conditon where the human agent becomes a passive recipient of information rather than an active constructor of knowledge. While the benefits of AI in promoting intellectual ability are evident, its limitations underscore the need for critical engagement. Studies reveal that students often exhibit superficial understanding when over-relying on AI-generated responses. This phenomenon, termed cognitive Offloading raises questions about the sustainability of intellectual growth in AI-rich environments [8].

By analyzing this phenomenon through Ernest Sosa’s framework of epistemic agency and his AAA model (Accuracy, Adroitness, Aptness), this review looks into the risks associated with cognitive offloading and argues that relying on GenAI often produces results that, while accurate, lack the aptness required for genuine human knowledge [9].

Theoritical Framework

Sosa’s Epistemic Agency and the AAA Model

Ernest Sosa categorizes human mental life into three distinct regions: sufferings (passive states like pains or itches), funtionings (involuntary but rationally motivated processes), and endeavors (performances with a freely determined aim). Epistemic agency resides primarily in endeavors—the region of freedom where a human agent makes conscious decisions to acquire the truth [9].

Sosa evaluates these performances using the AAA model:

  • Accuracy — the performance reaches the intended goal of truth.
  • Adroitness — the performance manifests the agent’s competence or skill.
  • Aptness — the performance is accurate because it is adroit [9].

For a belief to constitute knowledge, it must be apt; the truth must be attributable to the agent as his own doing. The core risk of GenAI is that it invites users to bypass the adroit manifestion of their own competence, leading to a breakdown in aptness making them to trust algorithmic outputs without crosschecking whether they are truth [9].

The Outsourcing of Cognition and Epistemic Dependence

The prevailing societal trend is characterized as a complex spectrum of epistemological risks, which consists a gradual progression in our relationship with knowledge and information. This spectrum begins with casual usage, where individuals use AI tools for non critical tasks , often without critical evaluation or in-depth understanding. As this usage continues, it can evolve into reliance, where individuals use these tools for significantly critical tasks and for information in their decision-making processes. However, this reliance can escalate into over-reliance, where individuals heavily rely on these tools to a point they form the basis of their reasoning and do not analyze the algorithmic outputs as truth. The final stage of the spectrum culminates in epistemic dependence, a state where individuals lose the capacity to perform conscious tasks independently and exclusively relies on AI for reasoning. (Chavanayarn, 2023) [3].

  • Cognitive Offloading and Atrophy Research indicates that chronic reliance on AI tools promotes "cognitive offloading," where users delegate mental effort to external systems. This leads to the distributed atrophy model, which suggests that AI usage can shape and shrink our cognitive habits, diminishing memory encoding and metacognitive self-trust. Bai et al.,(2023) highlights that individuals with a low Need For Cognition (NFC)—those who find effortful thinking unappealing—are particularly prone to using AI as a quick fix, further diminishing their reflective thinking Bai et al. (2023) [2].
  • The Erosion of Intellectual Autonomy This is the outsourcing of cognition transforms humans from active endeavorers into passive functioners or sufferers. Kausar and Shabbir (2024) note that excessive automation undermines students' autonomy and critical thinking, as they become passive recipients who may not even check primary sources. This creates a state of epistemic deskilling, where the agent loses the ability to fulfill epistemic goals through their own experience or testimony. (Costa Júnior et al., 2025) [4].

Accurate but Not Apt: The Central Epistemic Risk

  • The Problem of Adroitness — When a student uses ChatGPT to generate a differential diagnosis or an essay, the resulting text may be factually correct (Accuracy). However, the student has not exercised their own epistemic competence or adroit reasoning to reach that truth. In Sosa’s view, the result is a mere doing rather than an endeavor.(Armitage, 2023) [1].
  • The Illusion of Knowing — Tankelevitch (2023) reports that GenAI decouples perceived competence from real performance, creating an illusion of knowing where users overrate the quality of their solutions while overlooking errors. Because the user did not struggle with the knowledge, they lack the reflective, judgmental belief necessary to own the truth.(Tankelevitch et al., 2023) [10].
  • Automation Bias and Grounding Flaws — Epistemic passivity leads to automation bias, where users accept preformed conclusions. This is dangerous because GenAI often exhibits conceptual and grounding flaws— hallucinations that appear plausible but are factually incorrect. A passive user lacks the adroit competence to identify these flaws, rendering their belief un-apt even if it happens to be correct by chance.(Kidd & Birhane, 2023) [7].

4. Risks of AI from the Socratic and Sosa-esque View

To move from passive dependence to augmented intelligence, the user must re-engage their agency.

  • The Agrippan Trilemma — This is a fundamental challenge in epistemology concerning how beliefs or judgments can be justified without falling into one of three unacceptable outcomes ( Circular Reasoning,. Infinite Regress, Arbitrary Stance).Sosa notes that arbitrary stances are rationally repugnant. Accepting AI output without justification is an arbitrary starting point that fails to solve the Agrippan trilemma of justification. To achieve knowledge, the user must find a rational basis for their judgment. (Sosa, 2013) [9].
  • The Socratic Solution — Tonekaboni and Soleymani (2026) proposed a Revisited Socratic framework to counter passivity. By using AI as a partner for Elenchus (critical questioning) and Aporia (productive doubt), the human user is forced to articulate their reasoning and defend their claims. This process shifts the interaction from "command-and-response" to a maieutic process where the human "gives birth" to their own understanding. This re-introduces Adroitness into the loop, allowing the final outcome to be Apt.(Hashemi Tonekaboni & Soleymani, 2026)
    However, much of the analysis relies on illustrative scenarios or case studies rather than controlled longitudinal experiment. This makes it difficult to determine the long term effects of AI dependence on analytical reasoning and intrinsic motivation. Questions also rise whether cognitive practices are universal to the human form of life or if they are culturally specific, and how AI might impact these potentially differing modes of human flourishing.(Ye et al., 2024) [6] [11].

Conclusion

In current society, the increased use of GenAI threatens to trap users in a state of epistemic passivity, characterized by cognitive offloading and the atrophy of critical skills. From Sosa’s perspective, the fundamental risk is the loss of aptness(knowledge that is accurate only because of a probabilistic algorithm rather than human competence). AI can enhance performance, however, it cannot substitute the responsible free judgment required for genuine intellectual growth. In order to mitigate these risks, educational and professional systems must prioritize conscious digital engagement and human-in-the-loop oversight, ensuring that AI remains a tool for augmented intelligence rather than a substitute for human mind.
